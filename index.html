Slide 02 / Foundation

Why Most Review Systems Fail

Common Breakdown Points

  • Objectives are too vague to verify.
  • No quantity or percentage is defined.
  • No evidence artifact is named.
  • No timeline is attached to completion.
  • Accountability is unclear.

What That Causes

  • Inconsistent coaching from leader to leader.
  • Inflated or deflated self-assessment scores.
  • Low confidence in evaluation fairness.
  • Review surprises and defensiveness.
Bottom line: If you cannot measure an objective monthly, you cannot coach it fairly.
Key Takeaway: Clarity is the starting point for fair coaching and consistent scoring.
Facilitator prompt: "Which failure point is most common in your team right now?"
Slide 03 / Quality Guidelines

Quality Guidelines for Supporting Objectives

Proof

If someone claims 100% complete, can they show evidence?

Quantitative or Qualitative

Does the supporting objective clearly indicate measurable progress?

Progress This Month

Can the employee demonstrate forward movement each month?

Key Takeaway: Quality checks improve objective quality before check-ins begin.
Facilitator prompt: "Any supporting objective that cannot be evidenced is not ready."
Slide 04 / Standard

The Objective Standard

Use the criteria below as guidelines when writing supporting objectives.

Number

How much, how many, or what percent completion?

Sprint Timeline

Each supporting objective should be achievable by the end of the sprint period.

Evidence

What tangible proof will verify completion?

Guideline: No number + no evidence = not ready.
Key Takeaway: Every strong objective includes a number, timeline, and proof artifact.
Facilitator prompt: Ask leaders to name valid evidence examples for 3 real roles.
Slide 05 / Rewrite Exercise

Vague vs Objective Rewrites

Vague
I want to be more helpful to the team.
Objective
By the end of this sprint, I will be able to lead morning huddles and submit accurate agendas and notes for each.
Vague
I will learn more about budgeting.
Objective
Attend budget review meetings and begin submitting written summaries by month three, while improving each month through this sprint.
Vague
I should improve communication.
Objective
Send weekly project status updates by Friday 3:00 PM consistently through the end of this sprint.

Slide 07 / Primary Objective 1

New Skill Development

What This Primary Objective Measures

  • Capability growth beyond current role baseline
  • Readiness for expanded responsibilities
  • Cross-training and learning application

Strong Supporting Objective Examples

  • Complete leadership module and submit certificate by end of sprint.
  • Shadow Finance 4 hours monthly through the sprint.
  • Lead one 15-minute team training by end of sprint.
Check question: Will this supporting objective increase future role readiness in a visible way?
Key Takeaway: New-skill objectives should produce visible readiness for expanded responsibility.
Facilitator prompt: "What evidence proves the new skill was applied, not just attended?"
Slide 08 / Primary Objective 2

Mastery of Existing Skills

What This Primary Objective Measures

  • Execution reliability in core responsibilities
  • Quality and efficiency improvements
  • Consistency over time

Strong Supporting Objective Examples

  • Reduce report turnaround from 5 days to 3 by end of sprint.
  • Maintain 100% on-time submissions for the full sprint.
  • Create and publish one SOP for weekly inventory by end of sprint.
Check question: Is this supporting objective tied to a current-role KPI?
Key Takeaway: Mastery objectives should improve current-role performance that can be tracked monthly.
Facilitator prompt: "What current KPI most needs improvement for this role?"
Slide 09 / Primary Objective 3

Culture & Character | THE AMAZIMA WAY!

What This Primary Objective Measures

  • Servant leadership
  • Christ-centered focus
  • Others above self
  • Purpose in every step
  • Excellence in execution

Strong Supporting Objective Examples

  • Lead two team devotions or huddles during the sprint.
  • Complete three cross-team encouragement check-ins monthly.
  • Implement one morale-strengthening action by end of sprint.
Check question: Can this behavior be counted and evidenced each month?
Key Takeaway: Culture and character are measurable when behaviors are specific and countable.
Facilitator reminder: Culture supporting objectives still require number + timeline + evidence.

Slide 11 / Strategic Actions

What a Strategic Action Looks Like

Strategic actions are monthly actions that move an objective forward and can be verified.

This month, I did [action + quantity], evidenced by [artifact], to support [progress toward this specific supporting objective].

Worked Strategic Action Example

This month, I submitted 12 weekly check-in reports with 100% on-time delivery, evidenced by the submission log, to support reporting accuracy and leadership visibility.

Strategic actions evolve monthly through coaching; they do not need to be fully pre-planned on day one.
Key Takeaway: Objective stays stable for the sprint, while strategic actions adapt month by month.
Facilitator prompt: "Can someone outside this team verify completion from the evidence alone?" If not, adjust the action statement until proof is obvious.
Slide 12 / Monthly Rhythm

Monthly 1-10 Scoring + Check-In Calibration

Check-In Flow

  1. Employee self-rates each objective (1-10).
  2. Employee shows evidence.
  3. Supervisor affirms or redirects.
  4. Agree next-month actions.

Calibration Rule

If roughly half of measurable work is complete, score should be near 5, not 8 or 9.

Practice Scenario

  • Objective target: 6 client follow-ups this month.
  • Completed: 3 with logs.
  • Discuss score and coaching response.
Key Takeaway: Monthly calibration keeps scoring fair and coaching predictable.
Facilitator prompt: "What evidence would justify moving a score by +2 points?" If evidence is weak, hold the score and refine next actions.

Slide 13 / Scoring + Evidence

Monthly Scoring + Evidence Checklist

1-10 Scoring Anchors

  • 3 = started, limited measurable progress
  • 5 = about halfway complete with proof
  • 8 = mostly complete, minor items left
  • 10 = fully complete with clear evidence

Acceptable Artifact Evidence

  • Attendance registers or lesson-plan books
  • Assessment summaries and score sheets
  • Parent call/SMS/WhatsApp communication logs
  • Signed supervision or coaching notes
  • Monthly report exports and dated checklists
Ownership flow: Employee drafts + tracks evidence; Supervisor verifies score monthly.
Key Takeaway: Shared ownership works best when evidence is simple, visible, and consistently reviewed.
