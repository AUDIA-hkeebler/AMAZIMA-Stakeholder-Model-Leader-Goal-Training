Slide 02 / Foundation

Why Most Objectives Fail

Common Breakdown Points

  • Supporting objectives are too vague to verify.
  • No quantity or percentage is defined.
  • No evidence artifact is named.
  • No timeline is attached to completion.
  • Ownership and accountability are unclear.

What That Causes

  • Inconsistent coaching from leader to leader.
  • Inflated or deflated self-assessment scores.
  • Low confidence in evaluation fairness.
  • Annual review surprises and defensiveness.
Bottom line: If you cannot measure a supporting objective monthly, you cannot coach it fairly.
Facilitator prompt: “Which failure point is most common in your team right now?”
Slide 03 / Standard

The Supporting Objective Standard

Before approving a supporting objective, require all four criteria below.

1) Number

How much, how many, or what percent completion?

2) Timeline

By what month/date should this be completed?

3) Evidence

What tangible proof will verify completion?

4) Ownership

Who is directly accountable for execution?

Approval rule: No number + no evidence = not ready.
Facilitator prompt: Ask leaders to name valid evidence examples for 3 real roles.

Slide 11 / Strategic Actions

Understanding Strategic Actions

Strategic actions are not pre-planned at sprint launch. They emerge through monthly supervisor conversations as employees work toward supporting objectives.

What a strategic action looks like when it happens: By [date], I will [action + quantity], evidenced by [artifact], to support [team outcome].

Worked Strategic Action Example

By Month 3, I will submit 12 weekly check-in reports with 100% on-time delivery, evidenced by the submission log, to support reporting accuracy and leadership visibility.

Leader Activity (8 min)

  • Review one direct report's supporting objective.
  • Discuss what a strong first-month strategic action might look like.
  • Identify what evidence would confirm completion.

Key Reframe

  • Strategic actions are identified during monthly check-ins.
  • Employees are not required to pre-plan all strategic actions.
  • This keeps coaching practical and reduces admin burden.
Facilitator prompt: “Can someone outside this team verify completion from the artifact alone?”
Slide 12 / Quality Control

Run the 4-Part Supporting Objective Quality Test

Evidence Test

If someone claims 100% complete, can they show proof?

Number Test

Does the supporting objective include a specific count/percent?

Timeline Test

Is month/date for completion explicit?

Support Test

Is required supervisor support clearly identified?

Peer-review activity: Swap supporting objective drafts, mark pass/fail for each test, and rewrite any failed line immediately.
Facilitator prompt: “Any fail means the supporting objective is not approved yet.”
Slide 13 / One Supporting Objective Example

How to Write One Supporting Objective (Completed Example)

New Skill Development (Head Teacher)

Owner: Head Teacher

Number: 4 teacher-observation cycles

Timeline: by Month 5

Evidence: observation forms + action notes

Completed supporting objective: By Month 5, the Head Teacher will complete 4 teacher-observation cycles, evidenced by observation forms and action notes.

Example strategic actions:

Strategic action 1: Complete 2 classroom observations in Month 1 and file signed observation forms.

Strategic action 2: Hold follow-up feedback meetings and submit dated action notes.

Mastery of Existing Skills (Teacher)

Owner: P4 Teacher

Number: 90% lesson-plan completion

Timeline: by Month 4

Evidence: signed lesson-plan register

Completed supporting objective: By Month 4, the P4 Teacher will achieve 90% lesson-plan completion, evidenced by a signed lesson-plan register.

Example strategic actions:

Strategic action 1: Submit lesson plans for 5 of 5 teaching weeks this month, signed weekly in the register.

Strategic action 2: Complete monthly review with head teacher using the lesson-plan checklist.

Culture & Character (Administrator)

Owner: School Administrator

Number: 2 encouragement check-ins monthly

Timeline: for 6 months

Evidence: dated check-in log

Completed supporting objective: For 6 months, the School Administrator will complete 2 encouragement check-ins monthly, evidenced by a dated check-in log.

Example strategic actions:

Strategic action 1: Complete one encouragement check-in with two staff members this month and record dates.

Strategic action 2: Share a monthly encouragement summary with the supervisor by the 5th working day.

Quick check: If one of the four pieces is missing, the supporting objective is not ready.
Slide 14 / Scoring + Evidence

Monthly Scoring + Evidence Checklist

1-10 Scoring Anchors

  • 3 = started, limited measurable progress
  • 5 = about halfway complete with proof
  • 8 = mostly complete, minor items left
  • 10 = fully complete with clear evidence

Acceptable Artifact Evidence

  • Attendance registers or lesson-plan books
  • Assessment summaries and score sheets
  • Parent call/SMS/WhatsApp communication logs
  • Signed supervision or coaching notes
  • Monthly report exports and dated checklists
Ownership flow: Employee drafts + tracks evidence; Supervisor verifies score monthly.
Slide 15 / Monthly Rhythm

Monthly 1-10 Scoring + Check-In Calibration

Check-In Flow

1) Employee self-rates each supporting objective (1-10).
2) Employee presents evidence artifacts.
3) Supervisor affirms or redirects objectively.
4) Agree next-month actions and support.

Calibration Rule

If roughly half of measurable work is complete, score should be near 5, not 8 or 9.

Practice Scenario

  • Supporting objective requires 6 completed client follow-ups by month-end.
  • Employee completed 3 with evidence logs.
  • Discuss: appropriate score and coaching response.
Facilitator prompt: “What evidence would justify moving a score by +2 points?”
